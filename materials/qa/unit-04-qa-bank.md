### E9
**[Easy] [Remember] [Obj 6]** State two design principles for effective legal PowerPoint slides.

**Answer:** Any two of: one main idea per slide; readable font size; high contrast; minimal text; consistent theme; purposeful (not excessive) animations.

### M7
**[Medium] [Apply] [Obj 6]** Sketch the recommended slide sequence for a moot court presentation (list slide titles only).

**Answer:** Title → Facts → Issues Presented → Arguments for Petitioner → Arguments for Respondent (or rebuttal) → Authorities Cited → Prayer/Relief Sought → Thank You / Questions.

### M12
**[Medium] [Apply] [Obj 9]** List four file-management practices for secure legal work on a shared lab computer.

**Answer:** Log out after use; save to designated user folder not desktop; use meaningful file names (client matter + doc type + date); avoid leaving confidential PDFs in downloads; back up to approved cloud/USB per institutional policy.

### H5
**[Hard] [Create] [Obj 6]** Design specifications for a 10-slide "Legal Awareness: Cybercrime" presentation including where to use SmartArt, a chart, and one animation—and justify each choice.

**Answer:** e.g., Slide 3 SmartArt (process: report → investigate → prosecute) for clarity; Slide 7 chart (bar: complaint categories) for data impact; Slide 5 single animation (bullet reveal) to pace definitions; avoid animating every slide; speaker notes for IPC/IT Act references; high-contrast theme for classroom projection.

### U4-E1
**[Easy] [Remember] [Obj 6]** What is the difference between a slide transition and an animation?

**Answer:** A transition is the effect used when moving from one slide to the next; an animation is an effect applied to objects (text/images) within a single slide.

---

### U4-E2
**[Easy] [Remember] [Obj 6]** Name three elements commonly included in a moot court presentation outline.

**Answer:** Any three of: title/team; facts; issues; arguments; authorities; prayer/conclusion.

---

### U4-M1
**[Medium] [Apply] [Obj 6]** You must prepare an 8-slide legal awareness deck on cyberbullying for first-year students. List the eight slide titles in teaching order.

**Answer (sample):** (1) Title (2) What is cyberbullying (3) Why it matters (4) Common forms (5) Legal/college remedies overview (6) What students should do (7) Myths vs facts (8) Help resources / Q&A.

### U4-M2
**[Medium] [Analyze] [Obj 6]** A teammate pastes a full pleading paragraph onto one slide. Explain two problems and how to fix them.

**Answer:** Problems: unreadable text density; audience reads instead of listening. Fix: split into issue-wise bullets; move full text to speaker notes or handout.

---

### U4-H1
**[Hard] [Create] [Obj 6]** Design a 10-slide moot structure for a hypothetical constitutional rights problem (titles only) and justify why SmartArt or a chart would appear on at most two slides.

**Answer:** Structure: Title; Jurisdiction; Facts I; Facts II; Issues; Appellant arguments; Respondent arguments; Key authorities; Prayer; Thank you. SmartArt/chart limited because advocacy needs clarity; diagrams only for timeline or comparison—not decoration.

---

### U4-H2
**[Hard] [Evaluate] [Obj 6]** Critique the use of heavy animations in a courtroom-skills seminar presentation.

**Answer:** Heavy animations distract from legal reasoning, reduce professionalism, and may hinder accessibility. Prefer subtle builds to reveal issues one-by-one; prioritize contrast, structure, and accurate authorities.
